Hi, I'm **Pradeep Gangadharaiah**, you can call me PG, as in Parental Guidance, although these days nobody does that anymore. The word Pradeep comes from Sanskrit, meaning light or lamp, a ray of hope, a bright beacon, or spiritual illumination. I don't know if I live up to that, but I try. A software engineer by profession and a hobbyist runner.

---

My first encounter with a computer happened during summer holidays when I was in 3rd or 4th grade. My uncle worked in the Pharmacy Department at a hospital in Bangalore, and they had a computer in the office. I don't remember much about the specs; I just remember being completely transfixed by it. Those summer visits turned into impromptu computer sessions: I played [Dave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dangerous_Dave) and [Road Rash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Road_Rash), poked around [MS-DOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MS-DOS), and discovered the DOS text editor. That last one turned into a small practical project: instead of buying printed stickers from the bookstore to label my school books, I'd type out the labels and print them on the office's dot-matrix printer. It felt like a superpower.

My parents eventually enrolled me in a computer institute near home. The curriculum was thoroughly practical: MS-DOS, Windows 95, Microsoft Office (Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Access) and at the very end of the course, a few precious minutes on the internet. I was hooked. I spent the next few years living for floppy disks and CD-ROMs, burning CDs with Nero, and endlessly customising Winamp skins. By the time I was in 7th grade, I knew I wanted to be a software engineer, though I had absolutely no idea that path would one day take me to the other side of the earth, far away from home.

---

I pestered my parents for a PC every year, and every year the answer was some version of "later." The day it finally arrived was the week after my 10th grade board exams, in April 2007. My father spent ₹50,000 (roughly $1,200 at the time) on a custom-built machine: an [Intel Pentium Core 2 Duo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intel_Core_2) with an LCD Monitor. It was the best gift I’ve ever received.

Around that time I had to make a choice about what came next. Most of my classmates took the standard two-year pre-university path before engineering. I chose differently: a three-year Diploma in Computer Science at [Siddaganga Polytechnic](https://spttumkur.ac.in/) in Tumkur. It was a more hands-on curriculum, and it meant I was writing actual code sooner. From there, I did a lateral entry into the second year of [Sir M Visvesvaraya Institute of Technology](https://www.sirmvit.edu/) in Bangalore (a [VTU](https://vtu.ac.in/en/)-affiliated college), completing a 3+3 year path to an engineering degree instead of the usual 2+4 years.

In those years I also got obsessed with hardware, cycled through Linux distributions the way some kids collect trading cards, and convinced my parents to swap the monthly PC magazine subscription for a dial-up connection. When Airtel upgraded every connection in India to 2 Mbps for free in 2008 (tied to their IPL streaming deals), that sudden bandwidth changed everything. Within a couple of weeks I had a website running on a free host with a .cc domain, cobbled together with PHP and WordPress. By the time I finished my degree, I had built projects with HTML, CSS, JavaScript, PHP, and Python, one of which turned into a web scraper that got me my first full-time job.

---

I spent about six and a half years at [Beautiful Code](https://www.beautifulcode.co/) in Hyderabad, joining as the second engineer hired. For a while there were only four of us, which meant I touched everything: full-stack development, DevOps, AWS infrastructure. When Docker and Kubernetes arrived around 2015–2016, we migrated critical applications to Google Cloud and containerised them. It was a formative stretch, the kind of early career experience where you learn fast because there's no one else to hand things off to.

I joined [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/company/values/tech-at-bloomberg/) in April 2020. The first six months were remote from India; the pandemic had made that the default. I relocated to London in early 2021, then moved to the San Francisco office a couple of years later, and in April 2024 moved to Fremont, CA, where I commute to the SF office now. There's a lot more to say about that journey (the work, the cities, the people) and I'll write it up properly one day.

---

The running came later, and from an unlikely place.

I had always hated running. Never ran more than half a kilometre in one go in my life. I wasn't on the track team at school or anything like that. I even used to tell people I would never run. I was an active kid, but I had no formal training in any sport up to that point.

When I moved to London in January 2021, I was in my own apartment on the 19th floor in the northeast part of the [Isle of Dogs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isle_of_Dogs), overlooking the Thames with the [O2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_O2_Arena) and Greenwich on the other side, and working from home almost every day. The pandemic had emptied out the office. Most days the view from my window was grey sky and river, and people running along the bank in freezing cold.

I had never experienced real cold growing up in South India. But one morning I looked out and thought: why can't I do that? Everyone out there does it. I'd already made a decision that I wanted to stay genuinely fit into my 70s and 80s: eat well, move regularly, build the kind of health that compounds over decades. Running seemed like a natural extension of that.

On January 23rd, I dragged myself outside. 1.77 miles in 18 minutes 49 seconds. I ran my first 5k on February 3rd (32:52), my first 10k on Valentine's Day (1:03:00), and my first half marathon on my birthday that October (2:34). My strategy in those early runs was embarrassingly simple: tell yourself to reach the next lamp post. Get there, pick another one. The goal kept moving, and somehow so did I.

I run three days a week, long runs on Saturdays, mostly road. Seven half marathons to date, fastest (1:40:59), and earlier this year I crossed the finish line of my first full marathon (3:39:28).

What keeps me at it is hard to explain clearly. Running has become meditative, something that has changed how I think about patience, about staying with discomfort, about the gap between where you are and where you're trying to get to. The lessons don't stay on the road. They show up everywhere.
