It's 5:40am on a Sunday morning, March 1st, 2026. I'm sitting in a shuttle outside [The Westin Verasa Napa](https://www.marriott.com/hotels/travel/sfona-the-westin-verasa-napa/), a bag of gear at my feet and a pre-race snack in hand, about to travel 28 miles north to Calistoga where the [start line](https://maps.app.goo.gl/xcEoegXHT5hsArs26) is waiting. The weather is perfect for a race morning, clear and calm, though a touch warmer than ideal. The kind of morning that makes you feel like things might actually go right this time.

That phrase, "this time", has been doing a lot of work in my head for months. Though this time I was confident I'd finish, I just didn't know how much suffering was on the menu.

Today, 1,944 runners are aiming to cross the finish line in Napa Valley. I intend to be one of them.

## The Ghost of London

About a year ago I stood at a different start line in a completely different setting, arguably the greatest marathon in the world. [London Marathon 2025](https://2025tcslondonmarathon.enthuse.com/pf/pradeep-gangadharaiah) was the 45th edition of the race, held on April 27th, 2025. 56,640 runners crossed the finish line that day. I was not one of them. London had a hot, sunny day, unusual for late April, and if you've ever been to London, you know that joking about its weather is a local tradition. Maybe the city had heard enough of that from me and decided to take revenge, Who knows. What I do know is that somewhere around mile 12, my legs refused to cooperate and I slowed down to stop and see if I could shake it off with a couple of stretches. A few seconds after I stopped, that was it and I wasn't able to move, so I made the call that every runner dreads: I stopped. DNF.

The [London course](https://www.strava.com/routes/3340238562613360862) goes through some of the most iconic parts of the city and [Tower Bridge](https://www.towerbridge.org.uk/) at mile 13 is the centrepiece of it all. I never got there. That hurt more than the cramps. All the training runs in the cold and dark winter evenings in the Bay Area, from the last week of December right up to race day, it all felt completely useless in that moment. Looking back, I made a lot of first timer mistakes and learnt the lessons the hard way. Though I'm happy that I could raise some money for the charity and am grateful for the opportunity to just be at that start line. I was planning to write about it properly, I even shared some thoughts on Instagram at the time, but never made the time to sit down and do it justice. Maybe some day!

## The Comeback Block

After London I took 3 to 4 weeks off, then eased back in with easy long runs on Saturday mornings for a few weeks, nothing structured, just keeping the legs moving. On July 27th, I ran the [SF First Half Marathon](https://www.thesfmarathon.com/1st-half-marathon/) with Gargi, a scenic out-and-back across the Golden Gate Bridge. That evening, I signed up for the Napa Valley Marathon.

The reasoning was simple. I wasn't sure if I'd get into London 2026 through the ballot and didn't want to bank on it or plan another transatlantic trip. I also didn't want to give up my goals because training for a full marathon is almost a full-time job. A couple of colleagues who had run Napa in the previous years spoke highly of it, the course, the organisation, the whole experience. [RaceRaves](https://raceraves.com/races/napa-valley-marathon-and-half-marathon/) ranks it among the best marathons in the US and the reviews backed that up. It's about 60 miles from home, it wasn't expensive to enter, and it fit the timeline well. Easy decision.

I started a 26 week training plan on [Runna](https://www.runna.com/v2) in the second week of September. Compared to London, my base was in a better place going in. I was hitting the targets on speedwork sessions and the long runs with pace goals built in were going well week to week. I want to write a separate post going deeper into the training at some point, but the short version is that things were clicking in a way they hadn't before London.

The [Berkeley Half Marathon](https://www.theberkeleymarathon.com/) on November 16th, 2025 was the B-race, a checkpoint more than anything else. My first time running it. I kept it conservative, no need to sprint a half when you're mid-block for a full, and the last thing I wanted was to pick up an injury. I crossed the finish line in [1:42:00](https://sites.chronotrack.com/event/89259/results/entry/75748185), a 10 minute personal best. That gave me a lot of confidence heading into the final stretch of training.

## The Plan

The goal was [sub 3:20](https://www.runna.com/pace-guides/3-20-marathon). The [Napa Valley course](https://napavalleymarathon.org/wp-content/uploads/2022_NVM_Map.pdf) is a point-to-point run south along the Silverado Trail from Calistoga to Napa, net downhill overall with rolling hills in miles 15 to 20. That profile shaped everything.

The pacing plan was broken into four blocks:

- **Miles 1:** Warm up, settle in
- **Miles 2 to 6:** Controlled and patient, 7:42 to 7:52 per mile
- **Miles 7 to 14:** Race pace, 7:33 to 7:40 per mile
- **Miles 15 to 20:** Effort-controlled on the hills, 7:40 to 8:15 per mile
- **Miles 21 to 26.2:** Empty the tank, 7:15 to 7:35 per mile

A mantra for each block to keep the head in the right place:

- **Miles 1 to 6:** Hold back, hold back, hold back
- **Miles 7 to 14:** Smooth and controlled
- **Miles 15 to 20:** Effort not pace, shorten the stride
- **Miles 21 to 26.2:** This is what you trained for

For nutrition, 7 [PF&H](https://www.precisionhydration.com/us/en/products/pf-30-gel/) gels, one every 4 miles, taken slowly, half then half over a couple of minutes rather than all at once. Hydration came from a [Salomon ADV Skin 5](https://www.salomon.com/en-us/product/adv-skin-5-lc13270/LC2464900?sizeId=LC2464900018) vest with two 500ml soft flasks, one with plain water and one with [LMNT](https://drinklmnt.com/products/lmnt-recharge-electrolyte-drink?variant=39275991629847) for electrolytes. I had one extra gel and the LMNT stick just in case.

Most runners rely on the aid stations along the course, which are typically spaced 2 to 3 miles apart. Some have plain water, some have electrolyte drinks, and most offer sponsored gels that may not be what you trained with. Later in the race you'll find cut bananas and oranges too. I didn't want to think about any of that mid-race or have to remember where each station was. Carrying my own nutrition and hydration meant I could just stick to the plan. I had trained with the vest on long runs up to 22 miles so it felt like a normal part of the kit by race day. And if I ran out of water or needed a top-up for whatever reason, the aid stations were always there as a backup. The one thing the plan couldn't fully account for was the last 4 miles. Up to that point in my life, I had never run farther than 22. Whatever was waiting beyond that marker was unknown territory.

## The Night Before

We left Fremont around 2pm the day before and pulled into Napa just before 4. Less than two hours, no traffic on 680, smooth drive. First stop was the race expo at South Yard at the [Napa Valley Marriott Hotel and Spa](https://www.marriott.com/hotels/travel/sfona-napa-valley-marriott-hotel-and-spa/). My bib had already arrived in the mail a week earlier so I just needed to collect the rest of the race pack. They handed over a backpack and a full-sleeve t-shirt, both really good quality material, better than what I was expecting from a race goodie bag.

We had pre-registered for the pasta dinner so we had some time to kill. Sat in the courtyard for a bit until the doors opened at 5. The spread was solid: salad, bread, penne pesto, spaghetti marinara, chocolate cake, cookies and wine. I skipped the desserts and the wine. Gargi wasn't running the next day so she had a glass of red wine. Two rounds of pasta later, carb loading was done.

The dinner was more than just food though. They spoke about the history of the race, the course, how well prepared the organisation is, including the partnership with [Kaiser Permanente](https://healthy.kaiserpermanente.org/) and the fact that there are doctors and first responders stationed throughout the course. One of the Kaiser officials who is a doctor himself, gave a short speech about why he was running the next day. He had run the race in the 90s with a close friend, both of them crossing the finish line hand in hand. His friend had passed away recently and he was running again to dedicate the race to him. The room went quiet for that one.

We happened to be seated at a table with a group from [RRCA](https://www.rrca.org/), a couple from the Bay Area in their 60s who had run Napa before and had actually met each other through running, a man in his late 60s from Oakland who was running this race for the fifth time. And then there was [George Rehmet](https://youtu.be/47SOgpM4a-I?si=McPQjwOh12D1GHyt), who was about to run his 200th marathon the next morning, accompanied by his partner. George started running marathons the year before I was born. Across from him was [Lisa Rippe](https://www.linkedin.com/in/lisarippe), the president of RRCA, from Minneapolis. She was pacing the 2:30 half marathon group and had 175 marathons to her name. The room was full of people like them, marathoners and ultra runners with hundreds of races between them. We shared some heartwarming conversations with this group of seasoned runners who made us feel welcome. We were at the right table.

I took some of the best advice of the evening from that table. The veteran runners told me to focus on finishing and that whatever time I crossed in, it would be a PR, since it would be my first. There will be plenty of time to chase a faster one next time. The man from Oakland specifically warned me about mile 20: it looks flat on the course map but it's a gradual uphill that catches people off guard. I made a mental note of it.

By 6 we decided to head out, said good luck to everyone at the table, and left. The hotel was about 10 minutes away. I prepped my kit, laid everything out, and got to bed by 8 PM. The alarm was set for 4:15am. The shuttle to the start line was picking up from the hotel next door.

## Race Morning

The alarm went off at 4:15am. My Garmin logged 7 hours and 30 minutes of sleep with a sleep score of 66 out of 100, not ideal, but fine. The nerves didn't really get to me this time. Having gone through race morning once before with London took the edge off. I knew what to expect and wasn't really thinking about much. All the hard work was behind me. The plan was set. The only thing left was to go execute it. What mattered more was the week leading up to it. I had been hitting 8 to 9 hours of good quality sleep all through taper, so the body was rested. The wait was over. In a couple of hours I'd be running.

Pre-race meal first. A banana, and 500ml of water mixed with [Liquid IV](https://www.liquid-iv.com/products/white-peach-hydration-multiplier-sugar-free?Flavor=White+Peach&Size=14PK&selling_plan=21425094692) for electrolytes, sipping slowly while I got everything together. Two [GU Stroopwafels](https://guenergy.com/products/gu-energy-waffle?variant=31169162870835) I had picked up at the expo the day before, my substitute for the bagel and peanut butter. Roughly 400 calories in and electrolytes replenished, that was enough to get to the start line in good shape. Then the morning routine: theragun on the calves and glutes to wake the muscles up a bit. Packed the vest with water, LMNT, and the gels in order, plus a [PF&H chew](https://www.precisionhydration.com/us/en/products/pf-30-chew/) for the 15 minutes before the start gun. Got into race kit, double checked everything, and headed out.

It was cold and dark outside. A race volunteer was standing in the hotel parking lot guiding runners to the shuttle pickup at the hotel next door. I joined the small group of early risers making their way over, and that was it, race morning was underway.

As I walked up to the shuttle, someone was saying goodbye to their partner at the door. I got on after them. The bus was almost full, a school bus, the kind I had been in once before on a tour through [Denali National Park](https://www.nps.gov/dena/index.htm). It pulled away at a minute before 5:40, a few more buses behind it, and spent the next 30 to 40 minutes making its way up the highway toward Calistoga.

The guy next to me was wearing the same Asics Edge Tokyo shoes in green. The two guys across the aisle were deep in conversation about their personal lives, spouses, work, all of it, loud enough that zoning out wasn't really an option. A few minutes in I decided to just do breathwork instead. Better to use the time to focus on the race than get pulled into someone else's morning.

The shuttle eventually made it to the start line. There were already plenty of other buses that had arrived before ours from other parts of the city. I waited until most people got off and then slowly made my way from the back to the front and stepped down.

The porta potty lines were already building, so I picked one and joined it rather than risk a last-minute scramble. It was chilly and foggy out there. I could see the sunrise behind the hills, soft light just starting to break under the clouds. Around 6:30, I stretched a bit while waiting in the queue. After that, I walked away from the crowd and found a quiet spot to warm up. There was an old, almost broken tarmac road leading off toward someone's vineyard. A few other runners had the same idea and were already out there warming up. Some of them had that lean, effortless stride that makes you think elite. I kept my head down and focused on my own routine, up and down twice, a mile at an easy pace, enough to get the legs moving without thinking too much about anyone else.

Warm enough, I made my way to the start corral. The pacers were easy to spot. The 3:20 group wasn't at the line yet so I settled in between the 3:10 and 3:35 signs. The pacers were from the [SF Run Club](https://sanfranciscorunningclub.com/), the same club I used to run with when I lived in the city. I didn't recognise anyone though.

## The Race

### Miles 1–8: Settling In

The gun went off at 7:30 sharp, shortly after the Calistoga mayor gave a brief speech. Just before that, the American national anthem played. It was the first race I had ever run where they played it. Something about the moment caught me off guard and I felt a small wave of emotion. I took my hat off following the cue from the runners around me.

And then we were off.

The first few miles were smooth. No weaving through the crowd, no jostling for position. I felt in control, settled, taking it easy just as the plan said. The 3:20 pacer group gradually pulled away, maybe 35 to 40 seconds ahead, and I let them go. Around me a small cluster of runners had naturally formed, all settling into the same rhythm. By mile 4, it genuinely felt like we were all going to cross the finish line together.

I took my first gel at mile 4 and sipped it slowly over a couple of minutes, comfortable holding a steady 7:40 to 7:45 per mile. A few runners around me stood out. There was a lady to my left wearing a Kazakhstan t-shirt and pink Adidas Evo SL Woven shoes. I have the same pair in Solar Blue. I almost said something about it but didn't.

As we approached the first uphill, I switched from pace to RPE to keep things simple, letting effort guide me instead of the watch. She seemed to notice I had slowed, glanced back looking for me, then realised what had happened and carried on. After the climb, I caught back up and we fell into step together again for the next 5 or 6 miles. At some point she gave me a fist bump out of nowhere, just a quiet encouragement. I appreciated that.

Around mile 8, I worked through my second gel. The course by this point was stunning. Winding through vineyard after vineyard, the road completely closed to traffic, patches of small crowds cheering as we passed nearby estates. It was still foggy with a slight breeze. Honestly couldn't have asked for better conditions.

### Miles 9–18: Into the Heart of It

Around mile 9 there was a good-sized crowd lining the road cheering runners on, and an aid station to the right. I drifted left to stay clear of the station and keep moving. Out of nowhere a camera operator with a gimbal started running alongside me, rolling for about 30 to 40 seconds. I half expected to see myself in some race day highlight reel on social media afterward, but honestly he was probably after a specific shot and I was just convenient. I never found out.

I never saw the Kazakhstan lady again after that either. The brief adrenaline from the camera wore off quickly and I dialed back down. Settled in with a new loose group for the next 2 to 3 miles and found a steady rhythm again.

Gel number three came around mile 12 and shortly after we were passing through the half marathon start area. The half marathon gun had gone off around the same time as the full, so the crowds and runners who had gathered there were long gone. A few race crew members were breaking down equipment and loading it onto trucks. Quiet stretch.

Tail end of mile 14 had a decent climb and by the time I reached mile 15, most of the people around me had either slowed down or pushed ahead. A gap of 60 to 90 seconds opened up between the groups in front and behind me and I was running alone for the first time. That was fine. Following the plan meant slowing here anyway. I settled into 8:05 to 8:15 per mile and held it.

Fourth gel after I crossed mile 17 marker. By this point I had one thing on my mind: side stitches. They had shown up more than once on long runs during training and I knew how quickly they could derail things. So I was careful, sipping water and electrolytes sparingly, not rushing anything.

I hit the mile 18 marker and noticed it was at least 0.3 miles ahead of what my watch was showing. GPS drift on a course this long is common, but it still messes with your head for a moment.

### Miles 19–25: The Wall

Mile 19 had some downhill and I used it, picking up the pace and still feeling good. I thought back to what the man from Oakland had said at dinner the night before and I could see the gradual hills rolling out ahead of me just as he described. I navigated through them, keeping effort in check.

Toward the tail end of the hills I noticed a large group gathering behind me, 20 or 30 runners moving as a pack. I knew immediately it was the 3:30 pacer group. As they closed in, another downhill opened up and I leaned into it, crossing the mile 20 marker at 8:06 pace. Still in it.

What I didn't know was that mile 22 was about to collect its toll.

By then I had slowed to around 8:40 per mile. Not a dramatic drop but noticeable, and runners who had been behind me for miles were now flowing past. I told myself: take it easy, get to the finish line, nothing else matters right now. That was the whole job.

I started to see others struggling around me too. There was a female runner in all black who kept pulling to the right to stretch for a few seconds before pushing off again. She did it three or four times over the next couple of miles. Each time she'd stop, I'd pass her, then she'd come flying past me again. I admired that actually.

Around mile 23 the 3:30 pacer group crossed me, moving with a kind of easy grace that made my heavy legs feel even heavier. Most of those runners had probably run Boston. You could tell from the way they moved.

My Garmin was showing estimate of 10% of stamina left. That was the number I had been dreading. Time to dig deep and stop listening to my mind.

I wasn't thinking about stopping. I knew I wouldn't. But I was running on fumes and I needed something to hold onto. I opened my phone and put on a playlist of old Malayalam songs from the 90s that I had been on repeat with for the past few weeks. [This one](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oUM6L-LR38s) and [this one](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=YC3ujn41g-I) in particular. I had actually prepared for this, set them aside specifically for if I hit the wall. Something about those songs just works, now that I understand a bit of Malayalam.

Between miles 24 and 25, I grabbed an orange slice from an aid station. Ate it without stopping.

### Mile 25.8–26.2: Calf Arrest and the Home Run

Somewhere around mile 25 I pulled out my phone mid-stride to take a picture. Empty fields, wild flowers blooming, golden light. That is when Gargi's message came in: "Where are you? Didn't see at the finish."

She had been so sure I would cross by 11am and hit the sub 3:20. I typed back "25.83, almost three" and hit send without fixing the typo. Didn't matter. I pocketed the phone and kept moving.

The crowd started building again as the finish area came into range. I crossed the mile 26 marker and thought: this is it. I tried to pick up the pace, get something out of the legs for one last push.

Within about 200 metres my calf muscles locked up completely. Both of them, at the same time, like someone had hit pause. I stopped dead. Reminded me instantly of that freeze-tag game kids play. Fortunately the runners behind me had enough space and nobody ran into me.

One of the volunteers noticed immediately and came over. I pulled out my AirPods. He looked at me calmly and said "Give it a minute." I said yes, thank you, and meant it.

I stood there for a moment, then started walking. Slowly at first, about 50 feet of shuffling. Then the muscles eased just enough to run. I broke into a slow jog, kept it going, and then I saw Gargi on the sidelines. I slowed down, gave her a hug, and turned toward the finish.

I crossed the line in [3 hours, 39 minutes and 28 seconds](https://results.svetiming.com/napa-valley-marathon/events/2026/kaiser-permanente-napa-valley-marathon/136/entrant?share=1).

The announcer caught my name on the way through: "Nice job, Pradeeeep! You did it, congratulations!" I threw a fist pump in the air. Couldn't help it.

## After the Line

After crossing the line I felt free. That is the only word for it. A sense of accomplishment that is hard to describe until you have been there.

I collected the medal from the volunteers at the finish, who looked like high school kids. Right after that there was an official finisher portrait taken, then I found Gargi and immediately started telling her everything about the race. I did cool down stretches while I talked.

![After the line — with the medal](/assets/img/posts/napa-2026/after-the-line.jpg)

I got chatting with another runner nearby who had badly blackened toes from his [Nike Alphafly 3](https://believeintherun.com/shoe-reviews/nike-alphafly-3-review/). I almost ran that race in the same shoe. I had been planning on it, but on my 22 mile long run I noticed the toe box was causing some real discomfort so I made a last minute decision to switch. I had been running in Asics for a while, the [Superblast 2](https://believeintherun.com/shoe-reviews/asics-superblast-2-review/), the [Gel-Nimbus 27](https://believeintherun.com/shoe-reviews/asics-gel-nimbus-27-review/), the [Novablast 4](https://believeintherun.com/shoe-reviews/asics-novablast-4-review/), so the [Edge Tokyo](https://believeintherun.com/shoe-reviews/asics-metaspeed-edge-tokyo-review/) felt like a natural fit. I did a 15 miler and a 9 miler in them before race day just to make sure there would be no surprises. Luckily, there weren't.

I did spot another runner in the white version of the Edge Tokyo whose toe was bleeding through the shoe. I have had that happen before, a [Nike Vaporfly 3](https://believeintherun.com/shoe-reviews/nike-vaporfly-next-percent-3/) did the same to me during a half marathon in 2023. Shoe fit on race day is not something you want to find out the hard way. I am sticking with Asics for a while. Love their lineup for both training and race day. Unofficial Asics hobbyist athlete, that's me. Sorry, [Hoka](https://www.hoka.com/en/us/)!

## Closing Thoughts

Everything I had believed in about the training plan had worked out. And now, on the other side of it, I could see clearly where I had left time on the table. I missed my 6th and 7th gels, which almost certainly contributed to slowing down in those final miles. A few other training mistakes too, things I will fix next time. But you learn these things by doing them, not by reading about them.

Still, I am happy with what I accomplished. Six months of training, three runs a week, dedication and hard work. Not proving anything to anyone but myself.

I am officially a marathoner now. And man, running a marathon is tough. That feels like an understatement. The last 4 miles felt like an eternity. On a normal day I can run 4 miles in under 30 minutes. Out there, those last 4 took me almost 40.

If you made it this far, thank you for reading. This was a long one and I appreciate you sticking around. If you are planning your first 5k/10k/13.1/26.2 or have any questions about the race, training, running in general or anything else, feel free to reach out. Always happy to talk running.
